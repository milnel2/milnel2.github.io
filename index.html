I'm an assistant professor of Computer Science at Macalester College in the department of Mathematics, Statistics and Computer Science. I received my PhD in Computer Science and Engineering in June 2018 from the University of Washington, where I was an NSF graduate research fellow who worked with Richard Ladner. My dissertation topic was on exploring touchscreen accessibility for children who are blind or have low vision. I graduated magna cum laude from Carleton College in 2008 with a B.A. in Physics, and spent three years working as a personal care assistant to a young man with a disability and one year working as a "Mad Scientist", doing outreach events to get elementary school children excited about science.

I am interested broadly in making STEM education and computer science more accessible for underrepresented groups. My research has focused on designing accessible programming tools such as Blocks4All and StructJumper, and I have participated in a number of outreach events, including teaching the Processing to Deaf and hard-of-hearing high schoolers at the Saturday Computing Experience and volunteering at the Empowering Blind Students in Science and Engineering workshop.

Outside of school, I enjoy hiking, biking, skijoring (cross country skiing with dogs), triathlons and roller derby.

Blocks4All

Blocks-based programming environments are a popular tool to teach children to program, but they rely heavily on visual metaphors and are therefore not fully accessible for children with visual impairments. We evaluated existing blocks-based environments and identified four major accessibility barriers for visually impaired users. We explored techniques to overcome these barriers and built a fully accessible blocks-based environment on an iPad that uses audio and spatial cues to help blind children learn to program.

BraillePlay Games

There are many educational smartphone applications available for sighted children, but very few of them are accessible for blind children. The BraillePlay games are accessible smartphone games for blind children to help enforce Braille concepts. The games allow the user to read and write Braille characters using only the touch screen and audio of a smartphone. The games give both audio and haptic feedback about the Braille characters being shown on screen. See the V-Braille Website for more details.

StructJumper

When blind developers program, they can use Screen Readers such as NVDA to listen to their code, which sounds something like this:

However, this means they do not easily have access to many of the visual cues that sighted programmers rely on, such as indentation or keyword coloring. We created an Eclipse plug-in that creates a TreeView of a program, which indicates the scope of each line of code to give blind developers a better idea of where they are in their code.

Tactile Graphics with a Voice

It is often difficult to fit all of the original text from an image on a tactile graphic in Braille form, and many people who are blind or low-vision are not fluent Braille readers. Tactile graphics with a voice is a system that uses QR codes in place of text labels on a tactile graphic and gives feedback to blind users on how to aim the camera to scan these QR codes through a smartphone application. This is part of a larger project, the tactile graphics assistant, which aims to make the images in STEM textbooks accessible.

  • Tactile graphics with a Voice: Using QR Codes to Access Text in Tactile Graphics
